About

A curious mind at the
crossroads of science and story.

I grew up studying physics and mathematics, which gave me a particular way of looking at things — with structure, curiosity, and a real hunger to understand how things work from the inside out. When I walked into Mass Communication at AMU, I brought all of that with me. The combination of scientific thinking and storytelling instinct is what makes my work feel different from someone who only studied media.

The best journalism doesn't simplify complexity. It finds the human being inside it.
What drives my work

I'm completing my M.A. in Mass Communication at Aligarh Muslim University, one of India's most historically rich institutions. I've maintained a CGPA of 8.25 across three semesters, topping in Media and Human Rights in my first semester and in TV News and Current Affairs Production in my second.

I've worked inside IndianOil Corporation's Corporate Communication and CSR department in New Delhi, where I wrote scripts, designed communication materials, participated in public engagement, and contributed to a CSR branding presentation now used at their Refineries Division Conclave 2025. Before that, a graphic design internship taught me discipline, real deadlines, and the value of visual clarity.

I don't just make content. I find the story that was already there, waiting to be told properly.
On storytelling

My work spans radio documentary scripts, student newsletters, self-directed short films, and corporate communication frameworks. Each project has taught me something no classroom alone could have given me.

The SSB Experience

Pressure as a teacher.

I've cleared the Combined Defence Services examination four times and sat before the Services Selection Board three times — at 14 SSB Allahabad and at 17 SSB Bangalore. The selection process runs through psychological tests, group discussions, narration tasks, and individual and group leadership exercises.

What the SSB gives you isn't something you can frame and put on a wall. It's a kind of clarity about who you are when things are hard — when you're being observed, when the group is pulling in different directions, when the right answer isn't written anywhere. I carry everything I learned through that process into every professional setting I walk into. It shows in how I communicate, how I lead, and how I hold up under real pressure.

The SSB doesn't test what you know. It tests who you are when it is difficult.
